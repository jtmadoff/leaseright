# LeaseRight — Design Brief

## What this is

LeaseRight is property management software built specifically for the lease-up phase of new construction multifamily. That's the 12–18 month window between when a building gets its certificate of occupancy and when it reaches stabilized occupancy (~93%). During this period, the developer is burning carry cost on a $50–150M asset every day it sits empty. The pressure is enormous, but the tools available to them are generic property management platforms that treat a brand-new 260-unit building the same as a 40-year-old garden apartment.

LeaseRight is $1/unit/month. Forever. No hidden fees. Payment processing costs are passed to the renter, not the operator. The pricing itself is a statement: this is a tool, not a platform tax.

## Who uses it

The primary user is a development-side asset manager or lease-up specialist — typically 28–45, financially sophisticated, managing one to three assets simultaneously. They report to investors and lenders who want to know: are we on track to stabilize? They are not looking for a "cool app." They are looking for a tool that makes them better at their job and makes their reporting easier. They care about clarity, speed, and confidence. They've used Yardi, RealPage, Entrata — and they find all of them bloated, slow, and ugly.

## How it should feel

Opening LeaseRight should feel like sitting down at a clean desk with exactly what you need in front of you. Not a wall of charts. Not a mission control room. Not a chatbot greeting you. A well-organized workspace that respects your time and your intelligence.

The closest analog is not another SaaS product — it's the feeling of a well-designed private portfolio report. The kind of document a boutique investment bank sends to a family office. Restrained typography, generous whitespace, data presented with context rather than decoration. Every number on screen should answer the question "so what?" without you having to ask it.

The product's intelligence — its suggestions, its pricing analysis, its market comparisons — should feel like they emerge naturally from the interface, not like notifications bolted onto a dashboard. The software should feel like it understands lease-up the way you do.

## What it must not be

Not a fintech app. Not a "modern SaaS dashboard." Not a dark-mode analytics platform. Not information-dense for the sake of appearing powerful. Not playful. Not corporate. Not decorated.

If it looks like it was generated by AI, we've failed. If it looks like every other property management tool with a nicer color scheme, we've failed. If a user opens it and thinks "this is just a dashboard," we've failed.

## The bar

The design should make someone pause and think: "this was clearly built by people who understand what I do." Not because it has the most features, but because every detail — the way a suggestion is worded, the way a number is contextualized, the way the interface stays out of your way until you need it — reflects deep domain knowledge expressed through restraint.

Beautiful because it's purposeful. Warm because the stakes are high and the user doesn't need more stress. Sophisticated because the user is sophisticated. Simple because the job is already complex enough.
